Leafcutter Bee

To line their nests, leafcutter bees will take small, circular sections of leaves and overlap them within a small found cavity. No need to worry about the cuttings damaging the leafs or flowers, as their strong pollinating power more than makes up for it.

Lifestyle

Life as a Solitary Bee

Leafcutter bees are solitary bees, meaning instead of living in a hive with a queen like honey bees, every female leafcutter bee is fertile and the sole provider for their eggs. That is not to say that they live alone, as leafcutter bees are perfectly happy to live as neighbors in a home of hollow canes.

Super Pollinator

Leafcutter bees are incredible pollinators, as their whole fuzzy body is used to collect the pollen from flowers instead of just special carriers on their hind legs like honey bees. Thanks to this gardens can flourish under their care.

Life Cycle

Late Spring

Set out the nest late in spring so your bees have the whole season to grow and prosper. Place the hibernating cocoons close by the nest.

Summer

The males will emerge first, and then a few weeks later so will the females to begin mating. The females will gather pollen + nectar, and lay their eggs in unused sections of the nest.

Fall

When the summer flowers are no longer blooming, collect the nesting tubes with capped ends, and put them into your provided BeeProtection bag. Store them in a cool location for the time being.

Early Spring

Harvest the cocoons within the nesting holes and begin incubation about 1 month before the summer flowers that these bees enjoy bloom.

Nest

Your Leafcutter Bees Home

With your adopted family of leafcutter bees, we provide you with a nest specifically for them. Unlike Honey Bees that live in a hive, leafcutter bees prefer nests with hollow canes or burrowed holes. Our nest has hollow canes fitted within a wooden house frame. It can be easily mounted to the side of a house or on a tree. Leafcutter bees have a short flying range of 100 meters from their nest, so make sure flowers are nearby. The female bees will line the canes with leaves and lay their eggs within. They will also place food inside for when they begin hatching.

Flowers

Sunflowers

Tall and a lovely shade of bee attracting yellow, their a large and open faces are wonderful for bees. Sunflowers are a go to when it comes to giving your bees a rich supply of pollen.

Lavender

Lavender has a lovely fragance that both people and bees can enjoy. Just make sure they are planted in a sunny part of your garden.

Cosmos

These flat, open flowers give easy access to their pollen for bees. Plant in a large group to be even more attractive. They also have the bonus of being easy to take care of.

Poppies

Ideal for BC's moist climate, all Poppies are attractive to bees. This open flower is laden with pollen, and will provide quite the meal for your bees.

In our adoption process we first ask you about your region to make sure only bees that can thrive in your area are recommended. Due to this leafcutter bees may not be suitable for you. Start the adoption process to figure out if leafcutter bees are native to you!
